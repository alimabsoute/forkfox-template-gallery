Five-star ratings are lies we all agree to tell. A restaurant earns 4.6 stars because the patio is nice, because the owner waves at regulars, because one reviewer was having a bad day and took it out on their entrée. The number tells you the place exists. It tells you the room was fine. It does not tell you whether the braised short rib is any good.

Every food app ever built ranks venues. Yelp, Google, OpenTable, Michelin. They assume you want to know about the restaurant. You don't. You eat a plate. The plate is the unit. The restaurant is the address where the unit lives. ForkFox scores the units.

Five attributes — flavor, texture, value, execution, context — combined into a 0-to-100 score that's personalized to your taste. A 92 for you might be an 87 for somebody whose palate leans different. The algorithm learns. The score adjusts. No venue averaging.

A star rating averages a thousand opinions about different things. A dish score is accountable to one specific plate.

How it works, quickly

First the algorithm reads — public reviews across Yelp, Google, Reddit, OpenTable, enthusiast forums — filtering for plate-specific signal and discarding venue gossip. Second it weighs each mention against the five attributes, with cuisine-specific weights (flavor heavier for spice-forward food, execution heavier for technical-floor cuisines like sushi and ramen). Third it personalizes to your taste. Fourth it contextualizes across cuisines: a 92 on a Neapolitan pizza is not the same creature as a 92 on pho.

What makes ForkFox different

One: we score plates, not places. Every other food app ranks restaurants. That ranking is an average of unrelated things. We rank the dish.

Two: the score is personal. Your 92 is not your neighbor's 92. Every serious recommendation system since Spotify has worked this way. Food apps just never caught up.

Three: no incentives to lie. We don't take money from restaurants. We don't run ads. The beta is free. Win or lose on the quality of the scoring.

That's the premise. The rest is beta users, plate by plate, helping us sharpen what good actually looks like in a neighborhood we haven't eaten in yet. If you're in SF or Philly, the app wants to know what you're eating tonight.
