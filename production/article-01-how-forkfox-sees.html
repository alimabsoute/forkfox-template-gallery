The star problem

Every time you've opened a food app, it's handed you a venue. Four and a half stars. Save it. Map it. Drive there. Then you sat down and the dish that was supposed to be the signature — the thing the reviewers cried about — arrived cold, oily, or worse: mediocre.

A star rating is an average of a thousand opinions about different things. It can tell you a restaurant exists. It can't tell you what to order.

Frequently asked

Why dishes instead of restaurants?
Because you eat dishes, not restaurants. A venue rating averages the room, the service, and a dozen plates you'll never order.
How does the scoring actually work?
Five attributes — flavor, texture, value, execution, context — each scored from aggregated review data, then weighted and personalized to your taste profile.
What counts as "execution"?
Consistency across visits, technical competence of the prep, how close the plate matches the cuisine's canonical version. We weight this higher for cuisines with high technical floors (ramen, sushi).
How is this different from Google/Yelp?
Those rank places. We rank plates. A 4-star restaurant in our system might have one 94-scoring dish and one 62-scoring dish on the same menu.
Where can I try it?
iOS beta, free, live in San Francisco and Philadelphia. Join from forkfox.ai.
