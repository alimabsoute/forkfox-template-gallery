ForkFox's launch, explained.

An iOS app that scores individual dishes — not restaurants. Now live in San Francisco and Philadelphia.

ForkFox Team·April 18, 2026·8 min read

Five-star ratings are lies we all agree to tell. A restaurant earns 4.6 stars because the patio is nice, because the owner waves at regulars, because one reviewer was having a bad day and took it out on their entrée. The number tells you the place exists. It tells you the room was fine. It does not tell you whether the braised short rib is any good.

Pull that thread and something strange happens. Every food app ever built ranks venues. Yelp, Google, OpenTable, TripAdvisor, Michelin — all of them assume you want to know about the restaurant. But you don't eat a restaurant. You eat a plate. That plate is what you remember. That plate is what you tell your friends about. And that plate is where every food app currently falls silent.

We built ForkFox because we kept getting burned. A 4.7-star sushi place where the hamachi tasted freezer-forward. A Michelin-starred tasting menu where the bread course was the only thing worth ordering. A Mission taquería with 400 reviews whose carnitas earned a 92 and whose al pastor earned a 67 — and the reviews never told us which was which.

How the algorithm sees a plate

Scoring a plate is harder than scoring a restaurant. A restaurant is a fuzzy average of everything — ambiance, service, price, the one time the waiter was rude, the one time the dessert surprised you. A dish is specific. A dish is either good or it's not.

Our algorithm does four things. First, it reads public reviews across Yelp, Google, Reddit, OpenTable, and a few dozen enthusiast forums, filtering for plate-specific mentions rather than venue gossip. Second, it weighs each mention against five attributes — flavor, texture, value, execution, context — combined into a 0-to-100 score. Third, it personalizes that score to your taste profile. Fourth, it contextualizes across cuisines: a 92 on a Neapolitan pizza is not the same creature as a 92 on pho.

We've been working on this model since 2024. It is not done. Beta users will find edges. Every correction you submit trains the model. The app is built to learn in public.

Two cities, sixteen neighborhoods, one question

Our beta is San Francisco and Philadelphia. Not because they're the obvious markets — they're not, really. LA has more restaurants. New York has more food media. But SF and Philly share something the other cities don't: they both have neighborhood food cultures that don't show up on food maps.

San Francisco's Tenderloin has South Indian dosas that score 93. Its Chinatown has dim sum counters that out-score half the city's Michelin-starred dinners. Its Mission has taquerías that invented a category. Philadelphia is the same story in a different accent — the Italian Market's Vietnamese corner, Baltimore Avenue's Ethiopian stretch, Fishtown's post-2020 BYOB wave, Reading Terminal's DiNic's counter quietly making the actual city sandwich since 1954.

The rooms we're watching

We're not ranking restaurants yet — that would violate the frame we just spent this piece defending. We are, however, mentioning them. These are the rooms whose plates we're scoring, and you deserve to know.

San Francisco. State Bird Provisions, Nopa, Foreign Cinema, Swan Oyster Depot, Zuni Café, La Taqueria, The Slanted Door, Mission Chinese, Tartine, House of Prime Rib. Beyond the famous names, we're deep in the Tenderloin's dosa corridor, the Sunset's izakaya row, and Chinatown's dim sum parlors.

Philadelphia. Zahav, Vetri, Pizzeria Beddia, Royal Boucherie, Parc, Vernick, Fork, Morimoto, and the Reading Terminal counters. Plus the Italian Market's Vietnamese row, Fishtown's wine-forward wave, and Baltimore Avenue's Ethiopian stretch.

You will see some of those plates score higher than you expect. You will see some of the famous names score lower than you expect. That is the whole point.

A tasting menu tells you about the chef. A har gow tells you about the kitchen.On what the algorithm actually measures

What makes ForkFox different

One: we score plates, not places. Every other food app ranks restaurants. That ranking is an average of unrelated things. We rank the dish. Your venue might have ten menu items. You get ten scores.

Two: the score is personal. Your 92 is not your neighbor's 92. The model learns your taste. Every serious recommendation system since Spotify has worked this way. Food apps just never caught up.

Three: no incentives to lie. We don't take money from restaurants. We don't run ads. The beta is free. When we eventually charge — and we'll tell you when we do and why — the revenue model will not involve sponsored placements. Win or lose on scoring. That's the deal.

That's the pitch. We'd rather you try it than read about it.
